Turning it into a paid gig
Here's the honest landscape: there are three distinct revenue models, and they have very different ceilings and friction.
Model A — Paid by the family, not the facility. This is the cleanest path. Adult children of residents routinely pay for "senior companion" or "tech tutor" services for their parent. Rates in the GTA run roughly $20–35/hr for companionship, higher for specialized tech help. She'd essentially be a private contractor. The volunteer work generates these clients—a resident's daughter sees her helping and asks "could you also visit my mom on Thursdays, I'll pay you." This is how it actually converts.
Model B — Paid by the facility as casual staff/programming. Harder. Facilities have tight budgets and prefer free volunteers or existing recreation staff. But some retirement residences contract out "life enrichment" programming. Lower probability, more bureaucracy.
Model C — Independent service business. She markets directly to families (not through the home) as a "senior tech companion." This has the highest ceiling but requires the most hustle—and notably, doing it through a facility she also volunteers at can create a conflict (homes often prohibit volunteers from soliciting residents directly).
That last point is the biggest landmine: most volunteer agreements explicitly bar you from privately soliciting the residents you meet as a volunteer. So the clean sequence is: volunteer to build skills and reputation → get referred to families outside that specific home, or have the family approach her → contract privately. Don't poach within the building she volunteers at.
